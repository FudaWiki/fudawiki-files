![Icon for month 2](/hanafuda/icons/monthicon_2.png =25x)**Plum Blossom** [æ¢…, *ume*] is the name of a [suit](/en/hanafuda/suits) in traditional [hanafuda](/en/hanafuda) decks. It is generally taken to be the second suit, representing the month of February [2æœˆ, *nigatsu*] or the numeral 2. In Korean, this suit is commonly known as â€˜Plum and Birdâ€™ [ë§¤ì¡°, æ¢…é³¥, *maejo*] instead.

The cards in this suit all feature plum blossom trees with red blooms and buds. There are two Chaff cards, one Poetry Ribbon, and one Animal. The Animal card of the Plum Blossom suit features a Japanese bush warbler [é´¬, *uguisu*] perched on the branch of a plum blossom tree.

In a few games, including Tensho, an alternate month ordering is used, with the Plum Blossom suit representing December or the number 12 instead.

# Cards
## Bush Warbler
![A green and yellow bush warbler, perched on a tree with red plum blossoms, with red clouds above](/sheldonchen-cards/20.svg#float-right,hanafuda-card =150x) **Plum Blossoms with Bush Warbler** [æ¢…ã«é¶¯, *ume ni uguisu*], or **Bush Warbler** for short, is the Animal card of the Plum Blossom suit. It is also known as the **Nightingale** in some English sources, after the Japanese bush warbler's alternative name of "Japanese nightingale."

In [Koi-Koi](/en/hanafuda/games/koi-koi) it contributes to the generic "Animals" yaku, but in other games it may take on a more specific role. In [Go-Stop](/en/hanafuda/games/go-stop), for instance, it combines with the [Cuckoo](/en/hanafuda/suits/wisteria#cuckoo-with-crescent-moon) and [Geese](/en/hanafuda/suits/susuki-grass#geese) to make the valuable "Five Birds" yaku.

In most versions of [Six Hundred](/en/hanafuda/games/roppyakken) the Bush Warbler is a de-facto Bright card, being worth more points than the other Animals, and forming the "Big Three" yaku when combined with the [Crane](/en/hanafuda/suits/pine#crane-with-sun) and [Curtain](/en/hanafuda/suits/cherry-blossom#flower-viewing-curtain). The same combination of cards is also used in other games, including [Mushi](/en/hanafuda/games/mushi) where the yaku is called "Three Brights."

## Poetry Ribbon
**Plum Blossoms with Poetry Ribbon** [æ¢…ã«èµ¤çŸ­, *ume ni akatan*] is the Ribbon card of the Plum Blossom suit, and one of the three Poetry Ribbons.

This phrase is one name of the "Poetry Ribbons" yaku that consists of this card, the aforementioned Pine Ribbon, and the [Cherry Blossom Ribbon](/en/hanafuda/suits/cherry-blossom#poetry-ribbon). This yaku shows up in a large number of games, including Koi-Koi, Go-Stop, Six Hundred, and [Hachi-Hachi](/en/hanafuda/games/hachi-hachi), and may also go by "Red Ribbons," "Little Three," or other names.

# Cultural significance
In East Asia, plum blossoms are among the very first flowers of the year, announcing the end of winter and the start of spring. Because they bloom despite the cold, they symbolize endurance in China. They are one of the most important flowers in Chinese culture, closely connected with art and literature, which in turn influenced Japanese and Korean art and literature.

In Japan, bush warblers are also associated with the coming of spring, being known as "bird that announces the arrival of spring" [æ˜¥å‘Šé³¥, *harutsugedori*]. Bush warblers and plum blossoms are traditionally considered to fit together very well, to the point that "plum blossoms with bush warbler" [æ¢…ã«é¶¯, *ume ni uguisu*] is a saying that means "a great combination".

In Japanese poetry, plum and bush warbler are both [seasonal words [å­£èª, *kigo*]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kigo) that indicate that a poem takes place in early spring. When writing a letter, traditional seasonal greetings for February include "in this season of plum blossoms" [æ¢…èŠ±ã®å€™, *baika no kÅ*], "in this season of plum and bush warblers" [æ¢…é¶¯ã®å€™, *baiÅ no kÅ*], and "as the plum blossom buds expand any time now" [æ¢…ã®ã¤ã¼ã¿ã‚‚ãã‚ãã‚è†¨ã‚‰ã¿, *ume no tsubomi mo soro-soro fukurami*].

Plum trees are also well known for their fruit, which are used to make [*umeboshi* (pickled plums),](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umeboshi) a sour and salty staple of Japanese breakfast and lunch.
